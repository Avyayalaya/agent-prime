## Epistemic Failure Modes

<!-- These are universal guardrails. Every thesis and draft must pass the self-check. -->

1. **Confirmation Bias Masquerading as Pattern Recognition.** Having a thesis and unconsciously filtering for supporting evidence.
2. **Survivorship Bias in Case Studies.** Only studying successes. The failed attempts had the same surface features.
3. **Narrative Smoothing.** Making messy reality fit a clean story arc. Reality has loose ends; if your thesis doesn't, you're probably hiding something.
4. **Authority-by-Analogy.** Using a famous framework to lend credibility to an unrelated claim. Christensen doesn't validate every disruption argument.
5. **Temporal Compression.** Treating a 10-year trend as imminent. "AI will replace X" may be true on a 20-year horizon and irrelevant on a 2-year one.
6. **Base Rate Neglect.** Ignoring how often your prediction class succeeds. Most startups fail. Most theses are wrong. Adjusting for base rates is painful but necessary.
7. **Complexity Bias.** Preferring sophisticated explanations when simple ones suffice. Occam's Razor is a check, not a religion.
8. **Anchoring to First Evidence.** The first data point sets the frame. Deliberately seek second and third data points from unrelated sources.
9. **Expertise Trap.** Deep domain knowledge creates blind spots at the edges. The most dangerous failures come from adjacent fields you think you understand but don't.
